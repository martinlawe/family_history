Historical Context

Notes.

William was the eldest son of Sir Bernard Brocas and Joan (or Joan Midelton), daughter of Sir Thomas Midelton. He was born around 1379, probably at Beaurepaire Manor, near Sherborne St John, Hampshire.

His father's execution

This is perhaps the defining event of William's early life.
In January 1400, his father joined the Epiphany Rising, an attempt by supporters of the deposed King Richard II to overthrow Henry IV.
The plot failed, and Sir Bernard was captured and executed for treason on 5 February 1400.

Ordinarily, that would have meant the permanent loss of the family's estates.
Instead, Henry IV showed remarkable clemency towards the Brocas family.
William's mother retained her dower.
Within months, much of the family's property was restored. By 1404 William had been granted the remainder of the forfeited estates.
This meant that, despite his father's disgrace, William became head of one of Hampshire's leading gentry families.

Public life

William went on to become a respected Hampshire landowner and public servant.
Among his appointments were:
Member of Parliament for Hampshire
High Sheriff of Hampshire
Master of the King's Buckhounds (a hereditary office long associated with the Brocas family).

Unlike his father, William appears to have avoided political controversy and concentrated on rebuilding the family's influence.
William married twice. His second wife was Joan Sandys, daughter of Sir Walter Sandys, bringing additional property into the family, including Sherborne Coudray.
During his lifetime the Brocas estates once again became among the most important in north-east Hampshire.
The Brocas recovery One of the most interesting aspects of William's life is that he represents the recovery of the Brocas family.
The sequence is almost dramatic:
1330–1395 – Sir Bernard Brocas Senior rises to prominence as friend of the Black Prince.
1400 – Sir Bernard Brocas Junior is executed for treason.
1400–1456 – William patiently restores the family's wealth, reputation and estates. His descendants continue to hold Beaurepaire for centuries afterwards.
