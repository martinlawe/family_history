Historical Context

Simon Emmons was convicted in 1837 of stealing three bushels of clover seed, two bushels of trefoil seed, and two sacks from a granary in Willisham. For this, he was sentenced to seven years’ transportation. But what was happening in Suffolk at the time that might have driven him to steal?

🌾 Suffolk in the 1830s: A County Under Pressure

Understanding why Simon stole reveals more than just a crime — it shows the economic hardship and social injustice faced by rural labourers in early Victorian England.

1. Agricultural Decline & Economic Strain

  • The 1830s brought significant economic instability for agricultural workers.
  • Following the Napoleonic Wars, grain prices dropped, and farming profits declined.
  • The introduction of machinery (like threshing machines) reduced the need for manual labour, leading to widespread unemployment.
  • As an agricultural labourer, Simon belonged to one of the hardest-hit social classes.

2. The Swing Riots (1830–1831)

  • Labourers across southern England rioted against low wages and machinery replacing jobs.
  • Suffolk saw unrest and even arson in towns like Bury St Edmunds and Eye.
  • Although the riots had passed by 1837, the causes — poverty and insecurity — persisted.

3. The Poor Law Amendment Act (1834)

  • This act made poor relief far more difficult to access.
  • It introduced harsh workhouses, designed to deter reliance on public assistance.
  • Villages like Willisham would have felt these changes directly.
  • Without help, Simon may have turned to theft out of desperation.

4. The Value of What He Stole

  • Clover and trefoil seed were highly valued for farming.
  • Simon may have stolen to sell the seed for necessities, or to sow it for subsistence farming.
  • He didn’t steal luxuries — he stole tools for survival.

📜 Summary

  • Simon Emmons likely faced extreme hardship in a failing rural economy.
  • Support systems had collapsed, wages were low, and parish aid was limited.
  • His crime reflects deeper issues: poverty, inequality, and the collapse of the rural support structure.

🚢 Transportation to Van Diemen’s Land (Tasmania)

Simon was transported aboard the Lord William Bentinck in April 1838. He died in January 1840, just 17 months after arriving. What was the voyage like?

🛳️ Voyage Details

  • Ship: Lord William Bentinck
  • Departure: 10 April 1838
  • Arrival: 26 August 1838 (Hobart)
  • Duration: 135 days
  • Convicts Embarked: 320
  • Convicts Landed: 317 (3 died en route)

⛴️ Conditions On Board

  • Prisoners slept in hammocks or wooden bunks, often poorly ventilated.
  • Food consisted of salted meat, hardtack, oatmeal, and dried peas — fresh produce was rare.
  • Sanitation was basic: buckets for toilets, minimal washing facilities.
  • Diseases like scurvy, typhoid, and fever were common.

📚 Daily Life

  • Prisoners were expected to wash, exercise, and attend religious services.
  • On fine days, they were brought on deck for fresh air.
  • Some convicts were given reading material or assigned simple duties.

⚰️ Mortality & Aftermath

  • Simon survived the voyage but died in January 1840.
  • This suggests either lingering illness, exposure to disease, or the harsh conditions he faced upon arrival.

📜 Records Confirming His Journey

  • Surgeon’s Medical Journal — Dr. John Rankine recorded health conditions during the voyage.
  • Convict Muster Roll — Lists Simon as one of the men who disembarked in Hobart.
