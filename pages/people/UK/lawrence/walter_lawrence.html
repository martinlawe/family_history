Historical Context

He married Ellen Evans in 1879 in Watford where they had two daughters Mary Ann Florence and Gertrude Lillian. In 1883, shortly after the birth of Gertrude, they and emigrated to the United States, settling in Woodtick, Hartford County, Connecticut.

The couple went on to have a son, Walter S, in 1888, and by the 1910 U.S. Census, Walter was living with Ellen and their grandson, working as a painter and decorator.

In 1915, Walter returned to England, arriving in Southampton on 25th April, aboard the RMS Mauretania. Passenger records show he was travelling alone. The motivation for his return is not documented, but all available evidence points to a voluntary, personal decision rather than family duty, financial pressure, or declining health

Shortly after his return, Walter formed a relationship with Emily Archer, a woman significantly younger than him — in fact, younger than his youngest son in America. The relationship appears to have been deeply meaningful for both of them. Emily later told her daughter that the ten years she spent with Walter were the happiest years of her life. Between 1919 and 1925, they had three children together, including a son born in 1925 — the site owners' father.

There is no indication that Walter reconnected with his family in Watford upon his return. On the contrary, it seems he deliberately distanced himself from them, likely to conceal his past. Emily, too, was unaware of Walter’s previous life; it wasn’t until around 1925 — more than five years and three children into their relationship — that she discovered he was already legally married in America.

This revelation appears to have triggered a swift and decisive rupture. In 1926, Walter returned to the United States, again aboard the Mauretania. The reasons for his departure are not stated in the records, but the timing suggests he was either pressured to leave or chose to go rather than face legal consequences for bigamy in the UK — a crime that, at the time, could carry a sentence of up to seven years’ imprisonment.

Back in the United States, Ellen Lawrence was still living in Connecticut. She appears in the 1920 U.S. Census described as a widow — despite Walter being very much alive — which may reflect either her belief or a convenient explanation to authorities. Interestingly, in the 1930 Census, she is again listed as married. There is no evidence that she ever learned the truth about Walter’s second family in England.

Walter Lawrence’s life straddled two continents, two families, and two identities. His story is both a product of its time and a deeply human tale — of reinvention, concealment, connection, and ultimately, fracture.
