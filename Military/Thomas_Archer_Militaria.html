Family History

Thomas Archer

1893 - 1916

Military Service

Service Number:-9184
Regiment:- 1st Battalion Oxfordshire and Buckinghamshire Light Infantry



Thomas Archer served as a private soldier in the Oxfordshire and Buckinghamshire Light Infantry during the First World War. His service number, 9184, places him among the regiment’s regular soldiers, indicating that he enlisted before the outbreak of war or in its earliest months.

At the start of the conflict, Thomas was associated with the 1st Battalion, a professional unit of the British Army that formed part of the original British Expeditionary Force. Like many regular infantrymen, he would have undergone rigorous training and entered the war as part of a small, highly disciplined army that bore the brunt of the fighting in its opening phases.

At some point during the war — most likely in late 1915 — Thomas was transferred away from the Western Front. Such transfers were common, particularly for experienced soldiers, and often went unrecorded in later summaries. He was posted to Mesopotamia, a campaign that, although overshadowed by events in Europe, proved to be one of the most challenging and poorly supplied theatres of the war.

British forces in Mesopotamia suffered from extreme heat, inadequate logistics, and serious medical shortages. In early 1916, these weaknesses culminated in the disastrous siege and surrender of Kut-al-Amara, where a large British force was forced to capitulate to Ottoman troops. Thousands of soldiers were taken prisoner.

Thomas was among those captured and became a prisoner of war. Conditions for British POWs in Mesopotamia were severe. Many endured forced marches, chronic hunger, disease, and the long-term effects of exhaustion and malnutrition. A significant number survived captivity only to die later from illnesses contracted during their imprisonment.

Thomas died on 31 July 1916, approximately three months after the surrender at Kut. His death occurred at Basra, the main British administrative and medical centre in the region. This suggests that he may have been recovered from captivity but was already too weakened to survive. He was buried there, far from home, one of many men whose deaths resulted not from a single battle but from the cumulative hardships of the Mesopotamian campaign.

Thomas Archer’s story reflects a less-remembered aspect of the First World War: a conflict fought not in the trenches of France and Belgium, but in the heat and dust of the Middle East, where logistical failure and disease claimed lives as surely as enemy fire. His death stands as a reminder of the global scale of the war and of those whose sacrifices occurred far from the main theatres that dominate popular memory.
