Windsor has undergone significant change over the years, including the loss of entire streets and communities. This section aims to recover some of that lost landscape by identifying where earlier locations once stood in relation to present-day landmarks (as of 2025), and by documenting who lived there, in the context of our family story. We rely on confirmed sources such as census records and, to some extent, digitised church records, to ensure accuracy.

Our earliest recorded dwelling of a family member, excluding the D'oyly's and the Brocas', would be William Vickers, born in 1806 in Windsor. In the 1841 census, William is recorded living in Duke's Head Yard. This is believed to have been at the rear of the Duke’s Head public house, which stood on the corner of Oxford Road and Peascod Street—now the site of Creams, an Italian Gelato establishment. By 1851, William had moved to Garden Court, located where the River Street Car Park stands today, with probable access from Alexandra Gardens, beside the current Bowling Green.

Next we look at Henry Vickers. Born in 1840, he would have lived in Duke's Head Yard until the family moved to Garden Court. He remained there until his marriage in 1865. In 1871, he is found living with his in-laws, but by 1881, he had moved to 61 Victoria Cottages, Edward Square.

Henry's youngest son, Albert (born 1890), lived at 61 Victoria Cottages, Edward Square with his elder brother James after Henry's death in 1909. Following his marriage to Rachel Brown in 1919, Albert Vickers moved first to 66 Victoria Cottages and later to Clewer Fields.

It is worth noting that Victoria Cottages stood on the north side of Edward Square. Edward Square, along with Denmark Street, Grosvenor Place, Goswell Road, and Oxford Road, are all now part of the Ward Royal development.

Another branch of the family are the Browns. Isaac Brown was born in Windsor in 1817. He married in Wantage in 1841, and by 1851 was living in Grove Cottages, Love Lane, with his family.

John Brown lived in Love Lane in 1901, probably at No. 5 Wellington Square, which was off Love Lane. The family moved to various locations in Windsor, many are still being investigated, however we do know that Love Lane, Wellington Square, and Keppel Street were demolished to make way for the extension to Victoria Barracks around 1906. Rachel Brown, daughter of John, was living in Wellington Square in 1901 at the age of four. She married Albert Vickers in 1919, and went on to live in Victoria Cottages.

The Lawrence side of the family does not have the same long-standing connection with Windsor. The earliest record is the 1891 census, which shows Matilda Archer (nee Fowler) living in Sydney Place with her husband Thomas Archer (they married in 1890). By 1901, they had moved to 8 Victoria Cottages, Edward Square. By 1917, the family had moved to 10 Keppel Row, Sun Passage. Around 1925, Matilda was still living in Keppel Row, and it was here that her daughter Emily returned to after time away. Emily later moved to 4 Alma Cottages, where her son Leslie was born in August 1925. Matilda moved at some point before 1939 to 4 Helena Cottages, Clewer Fields. Helena Cottages no longer exist, but they once stood in Clewer Fields, along the footpath that now leads to Beaumont Cottages. They were situated on the east side of the path, while Napoleon Cottages—still standing today—remain on the west side. Helena Cottages were later demolished and replaced by new terraced housing.

We’ve explored several key locations connected with the family in Windsor. Keppel Row was situated in Sun Passage, which connects Peascod Street (to the north) with Victoria Street (to the south). That site is now the car park outside the library on the west side. Alma Cottages were at the western end of South Place, with access from Alma Road. The location is now where the entrance lies to the car park behind "The Hope" public house on Alma Road. Helena Cottages can be found in Clewer Fields which is a quiet, pedestrian-only footpath in Windsor, running east to west between Alma Road and Vansitart Road. It lies just beyond Ward Royal, west of Peascod Street, and runs parallel to both Oxford Road (to the north) and Bexley Street (to the south). The current houses, all situated on the north side of the path, were built circa 1890 following the demolition of older cottages as part of a slum clearance scheme in the late 19th century. The lane retains its enclosed, residential feel, with no vehicular access..

As a historical footnote, the family’s connection to Windsor stretches even further back. Robert D’Oyly, brother of Nigel D’Oyly, is recorded as having assisted in the construction of Windsor Castle in 1068.
