What Was the RAC Recce (1945–47)?

RAC Recce stands for Royal Armoured Corps Reconnaissance. It was a reconnaissance unit of the British Army tasked with gathering intelligence and scouting enemy positions. From 1945–47, many units were stationed in postwar occupied Germany and Italy, and others in British-controlled areas of the Far East.

  • Germany: Occupation, border patrol, security, and administration duties.
  • Italy: Support in post-fascist regions to stabilise governance.
  • Far East: British Empire reoccupation forces (Burma, Malaya, India).

The Blues and Royals (Postwar)

The name Blues and Royals was officially used from 1969 after the merger of the Royal Horse Guards (The Blues) and the Royal Dragoons (The Royals). Les may have served with one of these precursor regiments, and the term may be used retrospectively in documents.

🚙 Typical Equipment
In 1945–47, RAC Recce units would often be using:
• Daimler Dingo scout cars
• Humber Scout Cars
• Staghound Armoured Cars
• Sometimes even light tanks (like the Stuart) depending on the theatre
Les was a Trooper in the 9th Lancers, a Driver Operator, and was deployed to Montabello in May 1946. He returned to England in February 1948 from Egypt.
His Role: Driver Operator
In cavalry/armoured regiments like the 9th Lancers: 🔧 Driver Operator = Dual Role

Visual Reference

• Driver: Operated armoured vehicles (possibly a Daimler Armoured Car, Humber Scout Car, or similar light recce vehicle)
• Operator: Handled radio communications—vital for command and coordination during reconnaissance and patrol missions
He would have been part of a small crew, likely 2–3 men per vehicle, trained to:
• Maintain and drive the vehicle across rough terrain
• Operate wireless sets (like No. 19 set)
• Relay orders and intelligence from patrols to HQ
This was a highly skilled role—Driver Operators were trusted with both mobility and battlefield awareness. In a postwar setting, that meant frontline visibility, often out in the field while keeping eyes and ears open.
🇮🇹 Montabello, Italy (May 1946 onward) Context:• British forces in Italy were transitioning from combat troops to occupation/stabilisation
• The 9th Lancers would’ve been part of the British Forces in Italy (BFI)—keeping the peace, supporting Italian authorities, and patrolling volatile zones
Leslie's duties there may have included:
• Mobile patrols of roads and rural areas
• Convoy escort
• Monitoring partisan activity
• Serving as a visible peacekeeping presence
Northern Italy, especially in areas like Veneto (where Montabello Vicentino is), still had tensions from:
• Anti-fascist partisans
• Returning POWs and displaced people
• Friction between local political factions
🇪🇬 Egypt (Late 1946 – Late 1947)
After Italy, the 9th Lancers were likely moved to Egypt as part of the British garrison in the Middle East, probably stationed in or near:
• Cairo• Alexandria
• The Suez Canal ZoneDuties:
• Garrison duties and training
• Support for operations in Palestine (troubles were escalating there in 1946–47)

Egypt in this period was heating up politically—British presence was increasingly resented, and nationalist movements were rising.
🇬🇧 Return to the UK (Feb 1947)
By early 1947, many wartime soldiers were being demobilised under the "Age and Service" scheme. His return in February suggests:
• He likely received Class A release
• May have been processed through Woolwich, Catterick, or another demob centre
• He’d probably have been given a demob suit, pay, and rail warrant home
Les returned to the UK on 24 November 1947 from 156 Transit Camp aboard H.T. Otranto
🚢 H.T. Otranto – The Troopship
H.T. Otranto (short for Hired Transport Otranto) was a British troopship operated postwar by the Ministry of Transport. Originally a passenger liner, it was used extensively in the 1940s to ferry troops and returning personnel between overseas bases and the UK.
By late 1947, it was doing regular runs from:
• Middle East (Egypt, Suez Canal Zone)
• Far East
• India
...back to Southampton or Liverpool.
🛏️ 156 T/Camp
The “156 Transit Camp” was likely one of the demobilisation staging camps in Egypt, probably near Port Said or Tel el Kebir, both of which were major British military hubs at the time.
These camps handled:
• Final paperwork
• Medical checks
• Kit turn-in
• Issue of travel document
• Waiting for space on returning ships like the Otranto

This would have been the final stretch before Les boarded ship, after which it was a sea journey of about 10–14 days depending on the route. Historical Vibe: November 1947 To put this in context:
• Britain was beginning to pull out of Palestine (huge tensions there at the time)
• The Cold War was brewing
• Nationalist movements were pushing hard across Egypt
• British demobilisation was winding down, but thousands of troops were still coming home
Les’s return in late 1947 places him among the last major waves of demobbed WWII servicemen still on overseas duty—he served well beyond VJ Day, which adds to the significance of his service.
