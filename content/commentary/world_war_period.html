The World War Period in the UK and Ireland(1914 - 1945)

The first half of the 20th century was dominated by two devastating world wars that shaped every aspect of life in the UK and Ireland.

World War I (1914–1918) pulled in millions of men from Britain and Ireland to fight on the Western Front and beyond. Families were shattered by loss, and even those who returned were often changed by the experience. On the home front, women stepped into roles previously closed to them, a change that would slowly alter social expectations.

In Ireland, the war years overlapped with political upheaval. The Easter Rising of 1916, the subsequent War of Independence (1919–1921), and the Civil War (1922–1923) led to the partition of Ireland and the creation of the Irish Free State. These events divided communities and families, particularly in the North, where tensions remained high

World War II (1939–1945) again brought mobilisation and loss. Britain faced bombing raids, rationing, evacuation of children, and a total war effort that reached into every home. Ireland remained officially neutral but was deeply affected by the conflict — through the service of Irish volunteers in British forces, through wartime shortages, and through the political balancing act of neutrality.

Throughout this period, families adapted to constant change, uncertainty, and sacrifice. Many experienced dislocation, whether through death, evacuation, or emigration. The wars also led to the expansion of the welfare state in Britain, reshaping everyday life in their aftermath.
